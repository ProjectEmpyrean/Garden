Response:

> 1. Yes, Mara is explicitly described as dwelling in the Paranimmita Vasavatti realm in Buddhist texts, including canonical literature. He is portrayed as a devaputta (son of a god) ruling over this highest heaven in the sensual sphere (kāmāvacara-devaloka), often referred to as Vasavatti Mara or the king of the sensual realm (kāmadhātu-rāja). A portion of the realm is set aside for him and his retinue, where he embodies delusion, desire, and opposition to enlightenment. This association appears in sources like the Access to Insight compilations, drawing from suttas and commentaries, though early suttas (e.g., Saṁyutta Nikāya) focus more on Mara's actions than his precise abode, with the realm link often clarified in later exegeses.
> 
> 2. The Paranimmita Vasavatti (or Paranimmita-vasavatti) is the sixth and highest of the sensual heavens (kāma-loka) in Theravada Buddhist cosmology, one of 31 planes of existence described in the suttas. Beings here, known as devas, experience extremely refined mental and sensual pleasures without effort—they wield power over creations manifested by others (para-nimmita), meaning they enjoy sense objects (sights, sounds, smells, tastes, touches) conjured for them by lower beings or attendants, without needing to will them into existence themselves. This realm is a "happy destination" dominated by the five senses, offering long life, beauty, aesthetic joys, and certain powers, but it remains impermanent, subject to rebirth, aging, and death under the sway of Mara (who resides here as its most famous inhabitant).
> 
>    Direct references and inferences:
>    - **Description in suttas**: Scattered mentions in the Pali Canon (e.g., DN 11, AN 3.80) outline the 31 planes, with Paranimmita Vasavatti as the peak of sensual realms where devas "control what is imagined by others." Early suttas provide minimal detail, emphasizing rebirth causes over daily life; more elaboration comes from commentaries like the Visuddhimagga.
>    - **Lifespan**: Up to 16,000 "heavenly years" (where one day equals 1,600 human years), the longest in kāma-loka, implying immense longevity but still finite.
>    - **Existence type**: Blissful but tied to desire; devas here are spontaneously reborn (opapātika), free from gross needs like eating, but indulge in subtle sensual delights. Inferred risks include attachment leading to downfall, as the realm fosters complacency—Mara's presence symbolizes how even this paradise traps beings in saṁsāra. Rebirth here stems from ten wholesome actions (dasa kusala), generosity, virtue, and wisdom development (e.g., MN 41, AN 10.177).
>    - **Inferences**: As the apex of sensual existence, life is more refined than lower heavens (e.g., no self-creation of pleasures like in Nimmānarati), but proximity to rūpa-loka (form realms) suggests potential for higher insight if desire is transcended. However, Mara's influence implies temptation and obstruction for spiritual aspirants.
> 
> Early suttas indeed offer sparse details on the realm itself, prioritizing ethical and meditative teachings over cosmological elaboration—much of the vivid imagery comes from later traditions. If you're seeking specific sutta translations, those would require deeper textual dives.
